The Chromatic Clock

Some combinations of ingredients are notoriously difficult to balance and turn into a palatable dish, while other combinations work easily without much effort.

Combinations of pitch-ingredients, i.e. scales, share a similar fate: some scales are relatively easier to use as a basis for composition, while others require greater care.

How can we discover scales that sound melodious without much effort?

Constraints Catapult Creativity

By introducing constraints that favour stability and resolution, the scales we’d find would sound more like tuneful melodies in and of themselves.

An inadvertent side effect of this process would be a reduction in the number of possible scales, which I’m sure most of us would gladly welcome.

And once we bring this number down, we’ll be able to develop our intuition with just a few representative scales, instead of being sentenced to a combinatorially-clouded scale purgatory.

Simple Flavours

While Sa has introduced a sliver of resolution, there still exists the potential of chains of small intervals separated by bigger ones.

For example, the huge interval between ma and Sa in the scale above separates an otherwise unbroken chain of adjacent pitches. This makes for a tenuous and slippery melodic base.

How can we address this?

Ideally, what we’d like is:

  • to avoid long chains of small intervals, and

  • to avoid very large intervals.

If you imagine a scale to be a staircase that we’re ascending, we’d like the height of any particular step to be limited to a reasonable maximum, just like a real staircase.

At the same time, very much unlike a real staircase, we would like some variation in step heights. That way, any sequence of musical steps isn’t just one specific step height repeated monotonously.

What we’re looking for is a balance between simplicity and flavour.

One Of Each

Let’s notice that we can group 10 of our pitches into 5 pairs, or categories:

  • re, Re

  • ga, Ga

  • ma, Ma

  • dha, Dha

  • ni, Ni

Considering Sa and Pa as singleton categories, along with the 5 categories above, that leaves us with 7 categories of pitch in all (sound familiar?).

Our next constraint makes a categorical restriction on the pitches that can be used: a scale contains at most 1 pitch from any category.

So we can have Sa Re Ga ma Ni Sa', for example, but not Sa re Re ma Ni Sa'.

This gives us a drastic reduction, bringing the number of possible scales to less than 500.

In musical terms, imposing this constraint naturally removes scales that contain extremely big intervals. In addition, scales will no longer contain long stretches of adjacent pitches in a scale.

All this isn’t necessarily good or bad, and while we do lose out on some scales (for example, the 8-pitch Diminished scale), we’re steadily moving towards scales that are more beginner-friendly.

With the preceding constraint, we have inadvertently limited the maximum number of pitches in a scale to 7.

While you could theoretically have scales with less than 5 pitches, we can quite safely ignore these at present.
