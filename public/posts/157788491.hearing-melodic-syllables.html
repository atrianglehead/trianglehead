More Questions

There is a lot more nuance to pitch in terms of both variety and depth.

For starters, when listening to a melody without any assistance (in the wild, so to speak), how do we even decide which pitch is Sa?

Are there more pitches than the seven we’ve used?

What comes before Sa? What’s after Ni?

Are there scenarios that demand a different way of naming pitches?

Is there any connection between a Möbius strip and a Shepard tone?

More questions than answers: that’s how we know we’re learning.

While it’d be great to answer all of these right away, the present Melody series is only an introduction to decoding melody, and getting into these questions will divert us from our primary goal. I promise we’ll get to them, but for now, let’s answer a more immediate question…

Why decode pitch?

Why should we bother decoding the various pitches in a melody? I know the main reason I do it: it’s a lot of fun.

More practically speaking, it turns sequences of sound into more comprehensible “words”. These words then become the basis for remembering and understanding music, just like words in a spoken language.

If you think of pitches in a melody as the musical equivalent of spoken language syllables (sounds like “te” or “ja”), you can see how recognising them would make musical words so much easier to understand.

Acquiring this skill has helped me understand a vast array of musical patterns, laying the foundation for comprehending and composing the kind of music that I love. It’s like having a blueprint that allows you to peer into the inner workings of music, and I would wish it upon any music lover.
