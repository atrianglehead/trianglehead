Scales as Measuring Devices

Scales can be thought of as rulers — the measuring kind, not the tyrannically inclined.

Here’s our updated visual for the scale of Melody 4, from Part 8:

It uses two categories of intervals: large and small. In Part 8, we categorised all adjacent intervals of this scale by ear, noting that Ga-ma and Ni-Sa' were relatively smaller in size than the rest.

Pitch-Measuring Contest

Before going any further, let’s get a rough sense of how to determine interval size by ear. We’re doing this only to develop a basic sense of interval comparison, a skill that we’ll need very soon.

Listen to the following sequence of 2 pitch pairs (4 pitches in all). The first pitch in each pair is lower than the second. Can you tell which pair represents the larger interval?

If you could tell that the second pair of pitches contained the larger interval, then you’re absolutely right. If you found the task confusing, listen to the audio a few times until you can clearly perceive the difference in interval size.

Hearing Intervals

In the above audio, the first pitch of both pairs was exactly the same. This common starting pitch makes comparing interval sizes a little easier.

Let’s increase the challenge, by having no pitch repeating across pairs. Which of the following pairs contains the larger interval?

But this is just Melody 4’s scale. What about the other 3 melodies?

The scales of the 4 melodies from earlier, differ with respect to only 4 pitches: ma, Ma, dha and Dha.

That’s better. All neat and tidy. You can see that, compared to the scale of Melody 4, we have 5 new pitches in all: re, ga, Ma, dha and ni.

Well, it looks like we’ve filled in all the gaps of our original list. Or have we?

Could there be other pitches hiding in plain hearing?

Well, if you really think about it, there’s no theoretical limit to how many pitches you can find between any two other pitches.

How so? Let’s understand this with a number analogy.

Infinity Within

Here’s a number line:

Let’s zoom into the region between 0 and 1. If you want to find another number within this region, one simple way is to locate the number that’s halfway between 0 and 1.

That’s 1/2:

What if you wanted to find another number between 0 and 1?

Well, you could find the number halfway between the one you just found (1/2) and 1. That would be 3/4:

The next such number would be halfway between 3/4 and 1. That number is 7/8.

And by climbing this never-ending ladder of halves, we’d always be able to find another number between 0 and 1, or for that matter, between any two numbers. Zeno of Elea would be proud.

Infinite Pitches?

A similar logic can be applied to pitch, giving us a theoretically never-ending list of pitches.

Importantly though, we’d quickly reach a practical limit in our ability to hear such fine changes in pitch.

That said, before we actually reach these limits, we would actually find many more pitches than the 12 we’ve found so far. By introducing the ideas of temperament and intonation, we’d find ourselves excavating a deeper layer of pitch.

And while not impossible to do, introducing such perceptually-fine technicalities at such an early stage might be off-putting for many people (including me).

We will get to temperament eventually in a separate series, by which time it will be a practical tool in our listening/composing, instead of a theoretical nicety.

So, given the present practical limitations, for the rest of this series, we’ll limit ourselves to the 12 pitches that we currently possess.

Essentially, we’re focusing on the lowest-hanging fruits on the tree of pitch. This includes the 7 pitches that we know and love (Sa, Re, Ga, ma, Pa, Dha and Ni), along with the 5 that we’ve added in the course of this series (re, ga, Ma, dha and ni).
