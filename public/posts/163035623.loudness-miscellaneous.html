La La Land

There are 4 levels of loudness in this picture: Si, Ve, La, and Ha.

The central range here represents no change in loudness. The leftmost line is a decrease in loudness while the line on the right is an increase in loudness.

When we added a reference to the dictionaries of pitch and time, the reference was the leftmost line, which happened to be the shortest (zero length).

For loudness, we still choose the line with zero length to be our reference. The difference is that it’s in the middle this time. So the line to its right is of length 1 while the one on the left is of length -1 (minus one).

Why do we do this?

These ranges are all relative to silence, marked here as Si.

Loudness and Silence

Silence naturally comes up when we talk about loudness. It’s easy to think of silence as the absence of sound, but surprisingly, it also has to do a lot with our perception of sound.

If we encounter a pretty loud sound followed by a very soft sound, a lot of us wouldn’t even perceive the softer sound. Add to that the fact that, we’re almost always surrounded by background sounds that we don’t really notice while we’re actively listening to music.

Silence then, is better thought of as something in reference to the piece of music. where the loudness reduces dramatically compared to the sounds surrounding it.

This makes true silence something of a unicorn.

The loudness of

We should address something pretty important right away. When we talk about the loudness of X and Y, we’re talking about the stable level of loudness that persists for most of their duration.

The beginning and end of both these sounds have essentially zero loudness. Simply put, these sounds begin and end with silence.

Both X and Y have a part in the middle that is not silent. This loudness sustains for a while. This is the loudness that we are associating with X and Y.
