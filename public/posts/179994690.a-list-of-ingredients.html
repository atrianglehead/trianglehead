Melodic Dishes

If you think of a melody as a dish, its visual representation might be thought of as a recipe of sorts, since it’s telling us how and when to combine musical ingredients, and in what order.

In Part 5 of the Scale series, we compared the visual representations of 4 different melodies containing the same musical phrase (NiRe'Ga'Re'). These 4 melodies were the same ones we first heard back in Part 3.

Let’s now explore the question: what are the pitch ingredients of melodies?

Comparing Ingredients

Well, that’s no help. It’s no shocker that these visual representations, as they currently stand, are ridden with the same problem as their recipes: they’re visually indistinguishable from one another.

I see only one way out of this bind: we’ll need to use our ears.

Find the Double Agents

Let’s listen to the pitch-ingredients for both melodies in the same ascending order as shown above, from Sa to Ni. You’ll first hear Melody 1’s ingredients, and then after a short pause, Melody 2’s ingredients:

That’s better. Now we can compare ingredients.

Since the ingredients’ names are identical in both melodies, i.e. the first pitch in each list is Sa, the second is Re, and so on: our job is to find out which of these names don’t sound the same in both melodies.

Listen to the above recording as many times as you need and try to figure out: Which of the 7 pitch names are double agents, i.e. acting as a front for two unique pitches [Hint: It’s not Sa, Re or Pa]?

The differences can be subtle, so be sure to listen closely.

I’ll give it to you, and I’ll give it to you straight — there are only 2 double agent pitch names in these melodies: Ma and Dha.

What’s Next?

Ideally, we’d like ingredient naming to be a clean affair, with each pitch getting a unique name. In order to get there, we’ll need to clearly perceive the kind of multi-speak that’s begun to happen.
