Rhythmic Petals

Gati is one of those things that, simply put, changed the course of my musical life.

I had always been drawn to rhythm from a young age and fell quite in love with it in my late teens, especially with the more polyrhythmic aspects (multiple simultaneous rhythms).

Some years later, closer to my mid-twenties, I became aware of the perceptual element of gati (also spelled gathi), and other rhythmic ideas that naturally follow from it.

It was like falling in love with rhythm all over again, and I was able to experience the beauty of musical time more fully than ever.

I hope gati can do the same for you someday. Even if you’ll never be twenty again.

It’s such a simple idea, but like most simple ideas, it leads to a world of possibilities. In music, gati becomes the basis for almost any rhythmic phrase you might wish to express. Its power lies in its simplicity.

Perceptual Leaves

But what is gati?

In broad terms, gati is how you subdivide the duration between 2 adjacent tempo-beats.

Foundational Stem

To really get started with gati, it helps to have a reasonable grasp of tempo. If you’ve been following Sound Insight for a while now, you’ve probably already had some experience with it.

While we will most probably touch on aspects of tempo as they relate to gati in the course of the present series, making sure your foundations are in place will make the journey that much easier for you.

If tempo is a seed, gati is the first set of branches that arise on the stem of the maturing plant.

It’s the bud of tempo opening up to reveal the inner petals.

The leaves nourished by the root of tempo.

And thus concludes my ode to gati.

Human Roots

Ok, maybe it goes without saying, but — I am so excited to start this series! It’s really close to my heart.

Before we begin this next leg of our ongoing musical journey though, I would be remiss if I didn’t give credit to a major inspiration in my rhythm-learning journey.

More than a decade ago, a chance encounter with a book called Applying Karnatic Rhythmical Techniques to Western Music by Rafael Reina significantly altered my inner musical world.

In this book, I found a deep and systematic study of Karnatik rhythm techniques, which over the years, along with everything else I continue to learn about Indian and other music traditions, became a stepping stone to formulating the frameworks that I use in my own compositions and teaching, and which I’m sharing here with you.

The Forest That We Are

Of course, there’s a thousand other influences that have shaped me, but this book deserved special mention for having been a constant companion all these years. Even though I don’t really compose what would count as strictly Western music, nor do I really use Karnatik techniques in their original traditional contexts, I’ve found the style and content of the book to be deeply illuminating.

So, thank you, Rafael Reina, for the work you’ve done of putting multiple centuries of a deep and rich tradition into something an internet-fuelled brain like mine can comprehend.

And of course, a big thank you to every single musician, wherever and whenever you come from, because you are the giants on whose shoulders learners like us can stand and bask in the warmth of sound.
