How do musical composers and improvisers combine pitch, rhythm, loudness, noise and any other other elements of sound, in order to focus our attention and take us on an emotional journey?

I first had this question more than a decade ago, back when I still had a head full of hair. I kept asking myself: what do they know that I don’t?

All these years later, I can’t say I have anything resembling a complete answer. It is possibly too complex to be analysed by one person with their own biases and subjectivity.

Still, I have bits and pieces of a possible answer — enough to begin exploring the landscape of sound and using what I find to compose music.

At this point in my life, I’m more than happy with this, because it allows me to practically experiment with music, even if I don’t have a grand, unified theory of music to rely on.

Sound Insight is not only an attempt to communicate what I continue to learn, but also to fill in the gaps of my own understanding.

Analogies

There are a few analogies I keep returning to when I think about and teach music.

The biggest one is language.

explain the analogy.

But I find that this isn’t enough. So I rely on another analogy: magic.

I mean the kind of magic that professional performing magicians perform, illusions that surprise and bewitch the audience.

But that too, is not complete. Most people lose interest in a magic trick, once they know the method used to achieve the illusion.

With music, even once you know the underlying structure, you can still enjoy it. In fact, you might enjoy it even more.

This brings me to the next analogy: food.

There’s a recipe for a dish that you’d like to eat. If made right, and even if you know the recipe, you can fully enjoy consuming the dish.

Music can be enjoyed while you’re in the process of making it, and with recording, even after you make it. Music doesn’t need meaning and semantics to get in the way of enjoyment. It can surprise, create illusions and reveal deep emotions like other performing arts. And it can be conceptualised as having both mathematical and linguistic structures. It has connections to psychology, both in terms of intellectual, somatic and emotional effects.

No wonder it occupies such a fundamental place in human society.
