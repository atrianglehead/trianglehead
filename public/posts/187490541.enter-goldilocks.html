The Rhythmic Body

Tempo resides in, and is expressed through, the body’s movement.

Given the fundamental nature of tempo, it’s a good idea to learn how to move in sync with one.

I like to think of it as taking one’s first rhythmic steps. And those first steps become the basis for internalising the dance of rhythm.

Here’s the example for which we’re trying to get a rhythmic feel:

This is probably a good time to distinguish between internalising pitch and rhythm:

Pitch patterns are best understood by trying to replicate them with the voice (or an instrument).

Rhythm patterns are best understood by trying to replicate them with bodily movement.

So, I highly recommend clapping, tapping or performing any easy movement in sync with the rhythms you hear (including the example you heard above), to develop your rhythmic intuition.

The Goldilocks Principle

In Part 2 of the Gati series, we saw how tempo functions like an adjustable zoom, giving us the ability to understand a rhythm using just the right level of magnification.

So how do we figure out the exact tempo resolution we’d need to understand a rhythm?

Conceptually, it’s pretty simple. It’s the least level of zoom that ensures that every beat of the rhythm under consideration coincides with a tempo-beat.

Not too hot. Not too cold. Just right.

In our sapling-planting analogy, this would be like planting saplings so close to each other that very few survive due to resource-crowding.

When a tempo is too fast to keep up with, as in this case, we need to look at alternative ways of keeping track of a rhythm’s beats.

The Ruler of Time

We’ve seen and heard how a faster (or slower) tempo gives us greater (or lesser) time resolution.

We can also imagine tempo to be the gradations on a time-measuring ruler, using which we can measure rhythmic values.

Within this analogy, we’d like all of our rhythm’s beats to coincide with one of the ruler’s measuring marks.

So far, we’ve been using a ruler that has the same distance between adjacent gradations all along its length, i.e. just one tempo for the entire duration of the rhythm.

What if we used a ruler whose markings were spaced differently in different sections?

Essentially, we’d be using a different tempo for each section of the rhythm.

Tempo Stretching

But wait, what stops us from creating many such sections on this ruler of time, each corresponding to a different tempo?

Once again, practicality and usefulness will guide us away from the chaos of burgeoning tempo changes.

Our aim will be to have as few tempo changes as possible in the course of understanding a rhythm:

We will only use a different tempo when it is absolutely necessary.

In the case of our example, we’ve seen how using a single tempo to cover all the pattern’s beats leads us to a ridiculously fast tempo. It’s so fast that an attempt to keep up with its every beat with physical movement leads to either comedy or concern.

This is an excellent opportunity to introduce another tempo into the mix.

Relating Tempi

While it’s true that we’ve solved one problem, we’ve created another in its place.

Now that we have a tempo suited to each section of the rhythm, how do we relate one tempo to another in a concrete way?

When I say concrete, I mean that the mapping of relationships between tempi needs to be as clear as the relationships we’ve already established between beats (durations) and pitches (intervals).

The idea of gati is slowly and naturally taking shape here, but in order to conclusively reach it, we’ll need to cross this one hurdle.

Once we can do this, we’ll find the structure of gati instantaneously emerging from the scaffolding of tempo.

So, my question to you is: how would you relate one tempo to another within the same rhythmic pattern?
