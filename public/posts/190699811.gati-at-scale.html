Gati Partitions

Where there is a set, there is a subset.

Since a gati is an ordered set of beats between 2 adjacent tempo-beats, it naturally contains other (ordered) sets of beats within it.

This might ring a bell if you’ve been through the Scale series (specifically Part 11 and Part 12), where we were able to extract many pitch subsets (or scales) from the overarching chromatic set of 12 pitches.

Using only our homegrown species of gati flowers from Part 7, how do we find smaller “scales” within each gati?

Rhythmic Scales?

A gati sub-sequence has a relationship with its parent gati that’s similar to the relationship a pitch-scale share with its parent scale.

Unlike with pitch-scales though, the order of elements in a “gati scale” is bound by the flow of time.

For this reason, and also because we use rhythmic phrases to verbalise it, the more appropriate term for a “gati scale” would be gati phrase.

We can think of a gati phrase as a subset of the complete solkattu phrase we’d use for the full gati.

Solkattu Pronunciation Guide

Here’s a reference for pronouncing Ta, Ki and Ṭa at different tempi:

If that’s hard to emulate, here are some vague pointers:

Ta: Imagine you’re an Italian using a soft ‘tu’ in the word ‘tusk.’

Ki: just say the first syllable of the word ‘kimono.’

Ṭa: Imagine you’re an Indian using a hard ‘tu’ in the word ‘tumble.’

For linguistic nerds desiring greater precision (check the links for audio samples):

  • The consonant in Ta is represented by the IPA symbol ⟨t̪ ⟩, called the voiceless dental plosive.

  • The consonant in Ki is represented by the IPA symbol ⟨k⟩, called the voiceless velar plosive.

  • The consonant in Ṭa is represented by the IPA symbol ⟨ʈ ⟩, called the voiceless retroflex plosive.
