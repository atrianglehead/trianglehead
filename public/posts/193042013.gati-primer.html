Subdivide and Conquer

You’ve probably heard the koan: What’s the sound of one hand clapping?

If its creator, the 18th-century Japanese Zen master Hakuin Ekaku, wanted to incorporate the idea of rhythm into it, he might have phrased it thus: What’s the sound of one hand clapping in 4-gati while the other claps in 3-gati?

If you’ve followed the main thread of the Gati series, you might be able to tell that the above question is missing some information: there’s no mention of the tempo of each hand.

And that’s not the only thing missing. For example: When does each hand first start clapping?

We’ve seen how to address the first question in this series. The second one, though, is something we’ll take up in a future series, when we find ways to alter rhythmic emphasis.

Being with Gati

That said, we’d be better off taking a note from Zen, which prioritises action over talk. It is important to focus on the moon and not the finger pointing at it.

The goal of the Gati series hasn’t been to talk about gati and leave it at that. It is intended as a guide to come face to face with gati.

The experience of gati is the goal. I hope this series has helped you, the student of music, to find this experience.

Micro-Movement

Sometimes, I think of gati as a framework of micro-movement.

Usually, the tempo is such that it invites us to move in sync with it (think of the times when you tap your foot to a song).

But the subdivisions of the tempo, i.e. the gati beats, tend to be fast enough that it’s difficult to synchronise bodily movement with every one of them.

Still, gati is something that should be felt (and not counted). A great way to feel the gati is by using the verbal solkattu phrases for each gati. With just a bit of practice, these phrases allow us to experience each gati even at much higher speeds.

Just as pitches and scales give us glimpses into micro-moods, beats and their various groupings can give us the experience of movement.

Gatis, along with their allied phrases, can give us a window into quick movements, all embedded in a tapestry of regularly recurring beats.

Rhythm in Layers

Tempo is the foundational layer of rhythm. It sets up the foundation to perceive time.

The space between adjacent tempo-beats can then be filled in with faster subdivision beats, i.e. gati beats, to create a nested system of rhythm, like Russian dolls made of time.

There are other layers that we can (and will) include, but with only the 2 layers of tempo and gati, we already have a vast and deep system of rhythm.

If there’s nothing else you take away from this series conceptually, just remember that nested layers of beats come together as a unified tapestry of movement.
