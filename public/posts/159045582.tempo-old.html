Tempo

To begin with the obvious, it isn’t just one sound, but many.

Also, no two sounds overlap. There’s a first sound, a second one, and so on, until we get to the last sound.

In visual terms, we could mark sounds that come later as points to the right of sounds that come earlier:

[photo]

The sounds themselves seem identical to one another. So it’s hard to see any particular order to the sequence of sounds.

The sounds are separated by roughly the same amount of time. In fact, the time between any two sounds is exactly the same.

After we hear this sequence for a while, it becomes easier to predict when each sound will be heard. This sense of predictive ability is the cornerstone of rhythm. It is our ability to judge tempo.

There might not be any pitch here, but there is some regularity. Each sound takes the same amount of time to occur after the preceding sound.

This happens because, at the heart of it, pitch is composed of regularly repeating events, and by the time we reach such low “pitches,” we perceive the events themselves, and not the pitch they make up.

Regularity

What does it mean for something to be regular?

There needs to be a repeating pattern or some form of close similarity. For example, octaves occur regularly in the pitch spectrum.

One way to achieve regularity in the time spectrum, we’d need a sequence of identical events, with each event separated from its neighbours by the same amount of time.

Pitch Needs Regularity

There is more than one constraint that needs to be in place so that a sound is perceived to have a pitch. We’ll talk about just one of them here: time regularity.

In our example, these events happened to be the specific sounds we heard at the end. When these sounds follow each other at a breakneck pace (like the pitch we reached after a one-octave descent from Sa), we perceive their combination as a pitch. When the time between each of these underlying sounds is slightly longer, i.e. the sounds occur much slower (like the “pitch” we reached after a six-octave descent from Sa), we don’t perceive pitch but instead perceive the sequence as the individual sounds themselves.

This means that higher and lower pitch can be thought of as faster and slower pitch respectively. Slow down the sequence of underlying, regularly recurring sounds to get a lower pitch. Speed up the sequence of underlying, regularly recurring sounds to get a higher pitch.

Rhythm

Music can be thought of as being embedded in time. So the pitches of any melody end up being in some arrangement with respect to time. We can call this time-arrangement: rhythm.

We need to be aware of this time arrangement if we want to understand melodies. Understanding this arrangement becomes a lot easier if we have a framework of some sort.

Luckily, rhythm shares parallels with many of the concepts and visual representations we’ve already seen in the realm of pitch:

  • reference pitch: baseline Sa,

  • pitch dictionary: line dictionary,

  • pitches: points,

  • relative pitch: distance,

  • intervals: line lengths, and

  • Sa-octaves: Sa-lines.

With the framework that we’ll develop, we’ll be in a position to decode the rhythm of our melody. So let’s get started with rhythm!
