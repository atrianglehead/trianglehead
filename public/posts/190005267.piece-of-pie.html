Pieces of π

A gati divides the pie of tempo into slices.

The size of a slice of gati, i.e. the duration between 2 adjacent gati-beats, gets smaller as we squeeze more and more gati-beats in the space between adjacent tempo-beats.

Let’s call each small slice of gati a subdivision. The gati-beat at the start of a subdivision acts as its reference beat.

In Part 5, we saw how the duration between 2 adjacent tempo-beats, and therefore the gati, can be represented on a circle.

In this part, instead of focusing purely on the gati-beats, let’s also look at how gati slices the pie of tempo.

Gati Syllables

A great way to get accustomed to a gati is to say aloud a rhythmic phrase that contains as many syllables as beats in that gati.

These rhythmic phrases, collectively called solkattu, owe their existence to the rich tradition of Indian vocal percussion .

So, for example, we would say Ta Ka when in 2-gati, or Ta Ki Ṭa when in 3-gati.

Subdivisions and Beats

A syllable also acts as an identifier for the subdivision it references.

For example, in 2-gati, the Ta, which is simultaneous with the first gati-beat, is the identifier for the first subdivision, while the Ka identifies the second one (see above).

This is similar to pitch names identifying a pitch as well as its interval from Sa.

Parallel Frameworks

If you’ve read the Scale series, you might have noticed that the circular representations of gati and scale are nearly identical.

This is deliberate: we can think of gati as the rhythmic analogue of a scale.

Both scale and gati are like palettes of colours that we can choose from when composing music.

In addition, both use nested structures to create a relational framework of colours: tempo-beats and gati-beats in one case, and Sa-octaves and pitches in the other.

In this analogy, the duration between a pair of adjacent tempo-beats acts like a Sa-octave in the rhythm domain.

A Key Difference

And yet, there is one striking difference between how scales and gatis work.

Time flows in a single direction, so we can only ever go in one direction around the circle of gati. With scales though, we are free to select and arrange pitches in any order, jumping and changing direction on a whim.

What this means in practice is that if in the 3-gati, for example, we go past the Ki of tempo-beat 1, we can’t then include the Ta of the same tempo-beat 1 after it. That is, unless we time-travel to the past.

In the context of a scale though, we can use a pitch, let’s say Re, and follow it with a lower pitch, Sa, of the same octave, with no threat to the fabric of spacetime.
