Tempo as Panacea

Tempo awareness is the cure to most rhythmic maladies.

We’ve seen how a tempo’s beats can act as checkpoints in time, and also how a tempo can act as a reference for another tempo-like rhythmic pattern.

The reason a tempo is such a reliable reference is that all its beats are separated by the same duration. This makes it a predictably stable pattern in time, which almost all of us have the ability to follow without too much effort.

A Planter of Trees

Okay, now stay with me here. The following analogy is going to motivate the more technical exploration that will come after it.

A century ago, on the sides of a long, straight road between Town A and Town B were planted banyan saplings (Ficus benghalensis) at equal distances from each other.

Fast forward to the present day, and the road is not studded with equidistant trees as it should be — while some of the saplings have matured into large trees, the rest couldn’t make it and have long since perished.

Your first assignment in your new role as Assistant (to the) Regional Tree Planter is to plant saplings in place of the ones that perished, and turn the highway between Town A and Town B into a veritable canopied paradise.

Now, you don’t know the exact locations where the saplings that perished were planted a century ago, but you’re confident that you can use the existing trees to figure this out.

You also don’t want to plant more trees than necessary since, like most public departments, yours is pitifully low on funding.

You use a map of the area and try to figure out what the largest distance between trees should be so that the current hit-and-miss arrangement of trees is turned into a regular line of equidistant trees.

You try a number of distances, working your way from longer to shorter ones. Some work, but only for part of the arrangement, before one of the existing trees ends up at an inconvenient distance.

After much trying, you finally have a breakthrough!

At a very specific distance, you notice that every existing tree just happens to land at a multiple of your chosen length, and you achieve the optimal regular arrangement of trees without going over budget.

Maybe you see it already, but here’s how this analogy relates to what we’re trying to do with tempo:

The surviving trees are like the beats of the rhythm pattern we’re trying to understand.

The final arrangement of trees being aimed for is the tempo we use to understand the rhythm.

The distance between adjacent trees in the final arrangement is the duration between adjacent tempo-beats.

Town A is the start of the rhythm and Town B is its end, joined by the straight road that is time.

Which Tempo?

As for our example rhythm, let’s graph the first (and less chaotic) tempo selection along with the second:

Can you see how the choice of tempo impacts the degree of rhythmic chaos (or its absence)?

By tuning the tempo to be just right for the rhythm under consideration, we can expose the finer relationships that connect the rhythm’s beats, just like how selecting the right planting distance helped us map the arrangement of trees in our highway analogy.

In a nutshell:

The better choice of tempo is one which ensures that a greater proportion of its tempo-beats lines up with the beats of the rhythm.

Tempo as Zoom

In this way, we can think of tempo as the adjustable zoom on a microscope, theoretically enabling us to zoom in or out to any arbitrary resolution, with any rhythm pattern.

This is what allowed us to bring our example rhythm into focus, using just the right level of tempo-assisted magnification.

But — there’s a problem with our current choice of tempo.

If we want every beat in our rhythm to line up with one of our chosen tempo’s beats, then the tempo we’ve chosen for our example rhythm isn’t quite what we need.
