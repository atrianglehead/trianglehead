Laying Out Ingredients

How might we stock up and organise commonly used pitch-ingredients of musical recipes in a single place?

In Part 7, we got a taste for how substituting a one recipe’s pitch-ingredients can alter our listening experience. The lists of pitch-ingredients we used in the process were nothing but scales.

While a scale could be a haphazard assortment of pitches, we might be better off bringing some order into it. This will make it a lot easier for us to contrast scales with one another.

Scale Conventions

Here’s a first attempt at bringing some structure to the idea of a scale, while still leaving space for exploration:

  • A scale is a set of non-repeating pitches: The broadest conception of a scale is simply an arbitrary list of pitches. From this list, we remove repeats for the same reason that we list ingredients of a recipe only once: simplicity.

  • A scale is limited to a range of one octave: We do this because of what we know about octave-equivalence, i.e. that pitches differing by exactly one octave can be perceived as different types of the same pitch quality. For example, _Ga, Ga and Ga’ can be perceived as 3 octave-separated versions of one quality of pitch, Ga.

  • A scale’s pitches are listed in increasing order of pitch: Uniform ordering makes it easier to understand scales at a glance (or a listen). This also makes scale comparisons easier and quicker. For the sake of sonic completion, the scale ends with a pitch that’s 1 octave higher than the first pitch.

12 Pitch Palette

In Part 9, we settled on 12 roughly equidistant, octave-limited pitches, as our pitches of interest.

This collection, generally called the chromatic scale, is the parent framework that gives rise to all other scales (which use this system of 12 pitches).

It’s like having a palette of colours as an artist. You can choose different combinations of these colours, allowing you to express various moods.

In fact, we can make this analogy more literal by representing these 12 pitches as colours around a circle.

Scalar Circles

Arranging our 12 pitches on a circular clock-face like above gives us a convenient way to represent pitches and intervals in relation to the octave in a single, intuitive picture.

Especially, since we’re presently concerned with comparing scales and not as much with the precise intervals between each adjacent pair of a scale’s pitches, circular scales are very handy.

Mood Turns into Scale

We now have a robust enough framework to begin exploring different kinds of scales.

Within this framework, we can think of pitches as analogous to colours.

Similar to the influence a colour scheme can have on the mood of a visual composition, a pitch scheme — the scale — can influence the mood of a musical composition.
