Duration Naming Difficulties (Because Beats/Octaves contain each other)

When we were getting pally with pitch, you might have noticed that every set of 7 pitches (Sa to Ni) was separate from all the other octave-shifted sets of these 7 notes (for example the lower pitches _Sa to _Ni).

Each of these sets or octaves are discontinuous with each other. There’s no overlap between them.

When we come to the world of time though, things are not so cut and dry. As we’ve already seen in [Part xx], the rhythmic equivalent of a Sa-octave is a tempo-beat.

And within the bounds of what we’ve explored so far, the rhythmic equivalent of a 7-pitch set (Sa, Re, Ga, Ma, Pa, Dha, Ni) is a 4-duration set {Ta, Ka, Di, Mi}

Higher Beat/Octaves Become Reference for Lower Beat/Octaves (Like Descending Intervals)

How do we decode a rhythm like this one?

[photo of TaKaDi KaDiMi]

If you thought TaKaDi KaDiMi, you’re not wrong. But then how would you decode this next rhythm?

[photo of TaKaDi - KaDiMi]

Calling this TaKaDi KaDiMi would force us to consider the two rhythms above as identical, which they are not.

The way we’re going to solve this is vowel extension.

Every time we come across a tempo-beat (Ta), we will extend the vowel of the previous syllable. This will turn the first rhythm into:

[photo of TaKaDi iKaDiMi]

And the second rhythm becomes:

[photo of TaKaDi i iKaDiMi]

  • TaKaDi iKaDiMi → TaKaDi -KaDiMi

  • TaKaDi i iKaDiMi → TaKaDi - -KaDiMi
