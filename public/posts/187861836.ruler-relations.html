Relating Units

Gati is a system of units that relates one tempo to another.

For any given measurement, it’s helpful to have a system of units. And there are quite a few competing options when it comes to units.

Some prefer the foot to the metre when measuring distance, while others praise the Celsius scale over the Fahrenheit scale when measuring temperature.

On the other hand, you’d be hard-pressed to find someone measuring different parts/sections of the same quantity with varying units.

For instance, it’d be a rare occurrence to find someone expressing the length of a table as 2 feet plus 1 metre, instead of the more popular 1.61 metres (or 5.28 feet).

And yet, this is the kind of odd situation we routinely find ourselves in when trying to figure out the tempo of a rhythm.

One section of a rhythm may be better understood (i.e. measured) in terms of one tempo, while the next section fits nicely with a different tempo.

In such a situation, it is especially important to be able to relate one tempo to another, kind of like converting between feet and metres.

Equal Groups

Once again, tempo comes to the rescue. I wasn’t kidding when I called tempo a panacea earlier.

In order to fully appreciate how, we need to zoom out a bit.

Let’s try overlaying a slow tempo onto a faster one in such a way that the faster tempo-beats are essentially divided into equal groups:

This is exactly what we’ve done earlier when overlaying a reference tempo over another tempo, except that we’ve reversed roles by considering the slower tempo as the reference.

Equal, Unequal Groups

Now let’s try the same thing, but instead of overlaying a slow tempo onto just 1 faster tempo, we’ll overlay it on 2 sequential tempi, while still making sure that each faster tempo gets divided into simple groups:

We can see that the first tempo gets divided into groups containing 2 beats while the second tempo is divided into groups of a different number of beats, namely 5 beats.

At the same time, there’s only a single, slower reference tempo that’s doing this work of creating groups, with equal durations between any adjacent pair of its beats.

This essential property of tempo, that each pair of adjacent beats is separated by the same duration, gives us the ability to relate one tempo to another.

This single tempo that we’ve found gathers existing sequences of varying tempi into easily manageable groups, acting like a conduit or bridge between the various tempi.

It’s like having a ruler with just the right units enabling a conversion between 2 other rulers, each marked with its own unique set of units.

Layered Moving

We can think of our present situation as having a grand, unifying tempo that consists of groups of the earlier tempo-beats.

We now have 2 layers of tempo, mirroring the situation we’ve seen earlier in Part 12 of Decoding Melody.

  • an outer, slower layer of regularly-spaced beats, which we can call outer tempo-beats, and

  • an inner, faster layer of sequential tempi, whose beats we can now call the inner tempo-beats.

There are 2 major benefits to synchronising our movement with the outer tempo-beats:

  • With just one global reference, we can now move to the groove of a single tempo for the entire duration of the rhythm, and

  • Since this tempo is much slower, it is also easier to move with, and keep track of physically.

You can experience this for yourself by clapping with the reference beats in the audio above.

Gati in Plain Sight

We now possess a hierarchy of references to understand a rhythm:

  • at the top sits the reference or outer tempo, and

  • below is the inner tempo filling the space between outer tempo-beats.

The group of inner beats between a pair of adjacent outer beats is the gati!

I like to think of the tempo as measuring a larger unit of time, such as a day, while the gati marks a smaller unit of time, like an hour, nested within the larger unit.

The number of beats in any inner group, i.e. the number of beats in a gati, depends on the specific inner tempo in use at that point.

For example, we currently live in a world that uses a gati of 24 inner beats (i.e. hours) to divide tempo running at 1 beat a day.

At the same time, it’d be entirely possible to divide the day into 2 halves (gati of 2), 3 thirds (gati of 3), 4 quarters (gati of 4) or any number of parts depending on which gati suits our daily schedule the best.
