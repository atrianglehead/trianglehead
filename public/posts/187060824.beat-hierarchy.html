Rhythmic Perspective

There are 2 primary ways to perceive rhythm:

  • Duration

  • Frequency

In the preceding parts of the Gati series focusing on tempo and gati, we’ve constrained ourselves largely to the frequency side of things.

We’ll soon begin to incorporate the perception of duration using gati, but first, let’s take a look at what we have so far.

The Stillness of Tempo

Why are we using a nested set of time layers?

Firstly, here are some important reasons for using the primary (tempo) layer in the way we’ve set up:

  • Stable reference: The tempo acts as a stable, unchanging reference that stays with us for the entirety of the rhythm. Any changes are reflected in the secondary layer (gati) and not in the tempo layer.

  • Physical synchronisation: The tempo layer is slow enough that we can physically follow along with it by synchronising our movement with its beats.

This has implications for the gati:

  • Dependent, faster reference: Having the slower, static tempo layer frees up the gati layer to be faster while still being tied to the tempo, without worrying about how to physically keep up with it.

  • Speech-synchronised layer: In a way, the gati can be thought of as a more conceptual layer of rhythm, embodied as a group of beats rather than in an individual sense. To synchronise with a gati’s individual beats, speech — rather than limb movement — is frequently used, with syllables like Ta, Ka, Ki, Di, Ṭa, Mi and others taking the place of clapping/tapping.

Pecking Order

All in all, we have a hierarchy of reference beats, where:

  • the slower, primary layer is the tempo, and

  • the faster, secondary layer is the gati.

In summary:

The tempo is the slower set of regularly-recurring reference beats with which we explicitly synchronise our movements when feeling a rhythm.

The gati is the faster set of regularly-recurring inner beats that creates the scaffolding for every one of the rhythm’s beats.

Gati and Numbers

It’s common practice to refer to a gati by the number of beats it contains.

For instance, the gati in the first part of our example is 4, so we’ll call it 4-gati. Towards the end of the example, the gati then changes to 3, or in other words, 3-gati.

The number represents the number of beats of the gati (the secondary tempo) that are nested between 2 tempo-beats (primary tempo), inclusive of the first tempo-beat.
