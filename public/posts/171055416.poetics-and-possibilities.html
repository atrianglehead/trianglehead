I find composing music to be a lot like writing poetry. Each has its set of elements that come together in a variety of ways to create something greater than the sum of its parts.

While there exist poetic traditions that forego meaning and semantics entirely in the traditional sense (favouring combinations of sounds without regard to whether meaning emerges or not from these combinations), in the kind of poetry I’m accustomed to writing, meaning plays a major part.

I find it wonderful when the non-semantic patterns of poetry come together to enhance the layers of meaning that are being conveyed through semantic patterns.

The Central and The Peripheral

When a set of elements comes together, there are obvious patterns and non-obvious ones. In one sequence of elements, there can be many sub-patterns and parallel patterns.

Usually, there is one easily perceivable whole that becomes the central pattern. Other than this pattern, there are many others that may or may not enhance the central pattern.

The more patterns that come together to create a synchronous whole, the more it feels like the central pattern is strong and enriched, and the greater the effect on the perceiver of the pattern.
