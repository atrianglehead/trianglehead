You and I

Do you love learning? I certainly do. Especially if it’s about the foundational aspects of any discipline. Even better if that discipline happens to be music. I’m a music teacher by profession so hopefully I can be forgiven for the bias.

I’d love to share what I’ve learnt with you in a way that’s hopefully easy to follow. If something doesn’t make sense, please reach out. We’ll both learn something. So let’s jump in!

Let’s say you have a musical idea. Now what?

Scattered

For the longest time, once I had one musical idea, I would struggle to progress from there. It’s like my mind would remain stuck within the context of the first idea and render me powerless to find something that could connect before or after it.

This led to fervent prayers for inspiration to strike again, but more often than not, the next inspired idea would have no connection to the first one. This just made me feel more helpless because now I had two ideas from which I knew not what to do. The prayers invariably resumed.

Working with What I Have

Having taken enough spiritual beatings and possessing a long list of disparate musical ideas, I tried my luck with something else. I began to work on the ideas themselves and see how I could add to them or modify them so that they might connect to other ideas that I had.

This worked much better than prayer but still involved a lot of hair pulling. To give you a sense of how frustrating that was, I should tell you that I’m now almost completely bald.

Back to Basics

Finding that I lacked a clear perception of what made musical ideas tick, I turned to learning the absolute fundamentals of music, in the hope that I would clearly perceive the underlying Matrix of music and fly around like a musical Neo.

This turned out to be a big step in the right direction. I was able to quickly learn from the music I came across and could even identify how other musicians were creating a coherent whole in their music.

But — this was still at a level of musical input, and wasn’t completely integrated into my own musical output. While I had become better at musical analysis, my own compositional/improvisatory skills hadn’t kept up.

Know Thyself

Then, one day, like one of those earlier inspired musical ideas, it occurred to me: instead of limiting my analysis to others’ music, why don’t I analyse the spontaneous ideas that occur to me and find what is held within them?

The inscription on the Temple of Apollo at Delphi came to mind: “Know thyself.” There was another inscription that read, “Nothing in excess” — still haven’t fully implemented that.

Anyway, the musical ideas that had seemed disconnected at first began to open themselves up to connection in multiple ways as I kept trying to probe them. All those analysis chops from earlier now provided a framework for quickly dissecting what I had and I was able to find points of connection to other ideas — sometimes even new ideas.

The best popular analogy I can offer for this experience is the character of Muad’Dib, in Frank Herbert’s Dune, who sees multiple alternate paths to the future connected to present actions; the difference being that his character was also high af at the time.

A Journey Within

This is no silver bullet. In fact, I believe that every step of the journey that I undertook was necessary for me to reach this point. I’m sure there’s more to come that will make this seem like just another step in the longer journey.

What I am reasonably certain about at this point is that knowing one’s ideas in and out is an important aspect of coming up with related ideas. You also end up with a deeper understanding of your own musical mind.
