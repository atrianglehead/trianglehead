Since pitch decoding tends to feel more challenging for a lot of us, we’ll break it down into a bit more detail.

Throughout the process of decoding pitch, there are 2 questions we ask ourselves:

  • Q1: Is the current pitch higher, lower or the same as the previous pitch?

  • Q2: Which pitch from the pitch dictionary does the current pitch sound like?

For any pitch, Q1 is more easily answered by using the original melody (without Sa added in).

To answer Q2, the modified melody (with Sa added in) might be more useful, but you could use the original melody here as well.

We’ll begin with the first note of the melody:

  • Since there’s nothing before the first pitch, the question Q1 is irrelevant. We instead ask ourselves the question Q2:

    • Which pitch from the pitch dictionary does the current pitch sound like?

  • Listen to the first pitch of the melody and see if you can figure out which one of the 6 pitches from our pitch dictionary it matches. Take your time… Could you figure it out?

  • Drumroll… TaKaDiMiTaKaDiMiTaKaDiMiTaKaDiMi… The first pitch is: Sa.

We move on to the second pitch of the melody:

  • We begin by asking Q1:

    • Is the current pitch higher, lower or the same as the previous pitch?

  • If you’ve understood how to compare pitches [link], you should be able to tell that the current (second) pitch is the same as the previous (first) pitch.

  • This means that the second pitch is Sa as well.

Let’s move on to the third pitch:

  • Once again, we begin with the question Q1:

    • Is the current pitch higher, lower or the same as the previous pitch?

  • Hopefully, you can tell that it is higher than the previous (second) pitch.

  • This means that the current pitch is one of 5 pitches: Re, Ga, Ma, Pa or Dha, since these are the only possibilities in the pitch dictionary that are higher than the previous pitch Sa.

  • We now ask the question Q2, remembering that we only need to check 5 options:

    • Which pitch from the pitch dictionary does the current pitch sound like?

  • By comparing the sound of the second pitch of the melody to these 5 options in the pitch dictionary, you should be able to tell that it matches the sound of Pa.

Asking Q1 for the fourth pitch, we see that it is the same as the previous pitch Pa.

So far, our melody is Sa Sa Pa Pa.

In order to see how Q1 makes our lives much easier, let’s try decoding the fifth pitch of the melody:

  • We begin with Q1, and see that the current (fifth) pitch is higher than the previous (fourth) pitch Pa.

  • In our pitch dictionary, there’s only one pitch higher than Pa, namely Dha. So we know that the fifth pitch is Dha.

Q1 can vastly simplify the decoding of a pitch by reducing the amount of work we need to do. Without it, we might spend a lot more time comparing the current pitch to the options in the pitch dictionary.

Knowing if something is higher, lower or the same as the previous pitch can drastically reduce the space of options against which we need to check our current pitch.

So, we have the first 5 pitches of the melody: Sa Sa Pa Pa Dha.
