Discrete Time

Beats turn the the perception of continuous time into a sequence of discrete events.

It’s usually much easier to pay attention to discrete events than being aware of the continuous flow of time.

That’s probably why, during breath-meditation, many people report that it’s easier to focus on the start of an inhale/exhale (the beats of breath) rather than being aware of every moment of the breath cycle.

Beats in music capitalise on this biased attentiveness to discrete events.

Durations are still important, but their identity depends on the beats/events that enclose them.

Universal Gati Framework?

In the Scale series, we concluded with a 12-pitch circular framework from which we could easily select 7-pitch and 5-pitch scales.

Since beats are so vital to directing attention, could we maybe arrive at a similar kind of holistic framework that represents beats from all gatis in a single circle?

Well, yes.

Here’s how it might look if the pie of tempo were cut by all the gatis (2, 3, …, 7) we’ve looked at so far:

Unfortunately, this isn’t going to be very useful in most cases.

The reason is that in the overwhelming majority of rhythmic contexts, we need only a single gati between any pair of adjacent tempo-beats.

This means that, barring inevitable exceptions, in the duration between adjacent tempo-beats, we might have, say, 4-gati or 7-gati, but not both of them.

P(iv)ot

Given how well our circular gati representation connects tempo-beats and gati-beats, it would be really nice if we could still use it when transcribing rhythms.

This would mean we’d need to show the relationship between rhythms and their underlying tempo and gati(s).

In pictorial terms, this would involve showing each new gati of a rhythm in a separate circle, while still showing the onward march of tempo-beats.

A hidden bonus of using this approach is how it exposes some deep connections between individual gatis and pitch scales. But we’ll get to that when the time is right.

Tempo Flowerpots

So, where do we begin?

Coupled with an almost universal ability to maintain a rough tempo, our proclivity to notice discrete events leads quite naturally to the use of tempo as a timekeeping reference.

So now, just as before, tempo will be our starting point.

If we recall our tree planting analogy from earlier, we’d remember how tempo is like a row of beats planted at equal intervals on the fertile ground of time. The faster the tempo, the closer together the tempo-beats.

Let’s upgrade this analogy.

This time, we’ll begin with a row of empty flowerpots. The space between adjacent flowerpots represents the duration between adjacent tempo-beats.

Each flowerpot contains either:

  • a single plant, or

  • nothing

Gati Flowers

By design, every plant that we place in these flowerpots happens to belong to a flowering species.

But they share an even more notable trait: each species consists of plants that bear exactly one flower.

We’d like to be able to identify the species of any plant we see. Thankfully, there is a very simple way to do this. All we need to do is look at the flower it produces.
