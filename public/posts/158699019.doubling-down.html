We’ve come a long way from our humble beginnings. We have only one aspect of pitch to cover before we can move on to rhythm, or more specifically duration.

The one important aspect of pitch that remains is: the octave.

Now, in a way, we’ve already covered the octave (or Perfect Eighth) in a very general sense, since it’s an interval like any other (like the Major Third interval, which we’ve already encountered in Part 2).

Compared to other intervals though, it’s just a little more special. So special, in fact, that the topic of pitch would be incomplete if we didn’t talk about it.

Conveniently enough, exploring the octave will also allow us to transition smoothly to another fundamental aspect of melody: rhythm (or more specifically, duration). We’ll make this transition through doubling, the rhythmic equivalent of the octave.

Why single out the octave?

There are a few things that make the octave special.

First, let’s talk about how shifting a sequence of pitches (melody/harmony) by the same interval affects how it sounds.
