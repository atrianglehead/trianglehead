Music is a human endeavour. Just like language, it seems to play a vital role and resists codification. Each person involved in music gives something to it and takes something from it. These exchanges, both at an individual and societal level, change what music means to any given person in any given time and place.

Music isn’t one language. It’s composed of many musical languages. They each have their aesthetic and audience. And then there are the musicians and their allies: those who breathe and nurture music. They come together in a sonic kaleidoscope of their innermost beings, helping others experience their otherwise sliver of our shared reality. They release their own subjectivity out into the world to feed the subjectivity of others. As a musician, I can’t help but be in awe of the richness and diversity of these musical languages, some just born, some growing rapidly, some like wise geriatrics guiding the rest and some teetering on the edge of obscurity or present only in the ghosts of recordings.
