Back when I was in school, I would be terrified of the time right after the exam, right until we were told our exam scores. I hated that time because although I wanted to do really well, there was nothing (appropriate) that I could do to change my scores. The uncertainty was too much for me to handle, so what I did to escape it was to repeat the following maxim to myself: “No expectations, no disappointment.”

It helped at the time, at least for a number of years, until I could learn to accept reality for what it was.

I think this holds true in music and performing arts in general as well: if there are no expectations, there is nothing that can cause disappointment. There is also nothing that can cause its opposite either: no elation, no attraction, nothing to look forward to. For that matter, if there are truly no expectations, then there’s no reason to listen to music as well. You could just sit around and stare into the back of your eyelids for all it matters.

I don’t think it quite works like that though. Practically, unless you’re choosing to train your mind to go beyond the concepts of expectation and acceptance, pleasure and pain, there’s always going to be some sense of expectation at any given moment.

The reason my 4-word maxim was not truly worth it at the time was because I was using it as a way to escape my anxiety instead of facing it.

So when I think of terms like open-minded and accepting in relation to listening to new kinds of music, I don’t really buy it. I think that to be open-minded is to first be aware of our biases/preferences. The awareness of these aspects can actually help us connect to something new in a meaningful way.
