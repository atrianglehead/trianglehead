What Even is a Scale?

Is a scale like a list of ingredients? Or is it like a colour palette? A dictionary, perhaps? A summary of pitch variations? Or maybe it’s an indicator of mood?

Well, it’s kind of all of the above and more. It depends on how you look at it. And how you look at it can influence how you use it.

Perspective changes belief, and belief motivates action.

Mood Enhancers

Definitions can only take us part of the way, but an observation of behaviour and practical application can give a richness to motivation.

While the number of analogies in the Scale series might seem like overkill, I felt it vital to present multiple perspectives, so that we might practically incorporate scales into our music in multiple ways.

Even if you don’t have composition at the forefront of your musical priorities, scales can still be extremely helpful as tools for understanding music. They’re like blueprints that guide the structure of musical mood.

And it is this sense of codifying mood, towards which all the analogies are really pointing.

What’s Coming Up?

We’ll switch tracks and begin a new series that focuses on a crucial aspect of rhythm, gati. Over time, we’ll see how it functions somewhat like a scale in the rhythm world.

12 Pitches, 13 Parts

Going back to our often-used language analogy, we could think of scales as an introductory grammar of pitch combinations that allows us to express musically meaningful mood.

We saw how this kind of grammar might translate to phrases in poetry, visual illustration and melody.

We then built a framework of scales that divides the octave into 12 pitches. Using varying sizes of intervals, we found a way to select various pitch combinations to form scales embodying a variety of moods.

Along the way, we also began to represent the 12 pitches, and consequently any derived scale, on a circular clock with 12 pitch-colours instead of hours.

We introduced multiple constraints into the idea of scales so we could train our attention on just a few relevant scales and their accompanying moods.

Finally, we dived headfirst into recognising scales and moods in a musical context.

In a future series, we’ll see how a scale is a relatively vague indicator of mood, compared to the much more detailed blueprints that exist in the world of pitch.
