Now that we’ve established some concrete poetic examples, let’s explore the musical analogue of speech.

Vocabulary

If you’re comfortable reading English, it was probably quite easy to understand the poetry examples from Part 1.

A large part of this ease comes down to possessing a sizeable vocabulary.

To an extent, the more words you know, the better you can understand something expressed in a language.

Musical Languages

How can we find parallels to the above when it comes to music?

There are multiple musical “languages” in the form of musical traditions, styles and genres. Each has patterns that it shares with others, as well as patterns that distinguish it from others.

The more we listen to a particular kind of music, the more familiar and “understandable” it seems. The patterns in the music start to feel more predictable.

In this sense, musical acquisition seems similar to language acquisition. Greater exposure to a language combined with the desire to understand lead to greater fluency.

Shared Vocabulary

Languages rely on a shared vocabulary out of necessity, since if I don’t understand the words that you’re saying, communication will quickly grind to a halt.

There might be times when linguistic obfuscation is an intentional act, but in most cases, language users actively try to maintain a clear channel of communication.

A shared vocabulary certainly has its place in music as well.

Especially in improvisational art forms such as khayaal, dhrupad, karnaatik, flamenco or jazz, it pays for a musician to develop an encyclopaedic language-specific vocabulary.

Of course, this does not mean that these musical styles are in any way limited in expressive potential. If anything, they lean into their self-imposed constraints for greater emotive power, much like poetry.

When Languages Meet

That said, a musician, unlike a language user, can freely explore musical lands well outside existing traditions and still be well-received by listeners.

In general, I might positively relish your music even if I can’t fully comprehend your phrases.

And in our day and age, when we’re exposed to so many styles of music, many of them mixing to produce new ones, it’s understandably hard to pin down a shared vocabulary of musical phrases.

This leads us to a powerful general strategy if we want to learn multiple musical styles: instead of focusing exclusively on learning a list of musical phrases, we might be served better by understanding general patterns that underlie phrases.

This enables us to quickly pick up, combine and compose in a wider range of musical styles.

To put this into practice, let’s now try to break the musical phrase from above into smaller patterns.

Splitting Words

Phrases can be extremely short, extremely long, or anything in between.

A musical phrase then, can be thought of as a word in a language where such variance in word length is commonplace (Sanskrit, for example).

While the decoded version of the above musical phrase (Sa Pa Ma Pa Dha Pa Ma Ga Ma Ga Re Sa Ni_ Sa) looks formidably long, it becomes easier to understand once we break it down into sub-components.

Let’s try this on an English word before applying a similar process to our musical phrase.

The word that we’re going to split comes from a line taken from the fourth passage of poetry in Part 1:

Outside for anonymity.

Almost-Words

Let’s focus on the word “anonymity” from the above line.

It comes from the Greek word ἀνώνυμος (anṓnumos), which is “an-” + “-onoma”, or “without” + “name.”

The ending of the word is “-ity,” denoting “a state of being or quality”.

To keep grammatical technicalities to a minimum, let’s just call these bits almost-words. Put these together and you get a word.

Anonymity → “an-” + “-onoma-” + “-ity.”

In terms of meanings, anonymity → “without” + “name” + “state of being/quality.”

And that’s what anonymity means: the state or quality of being nameless.

Phrase vs. Almost-Phrase

This might be something that puzzled you: How do we know if something is a phrase and or an almost-phrase?

Well…for now, the easiest way to think about it is that it comes down to consensus and individual preference.

Loosely speaking, a phrase contains a complete musical idea while an almost-phrase is building up to a complete musical idea.

This can vary depending on the kind of music you listen to and/or create.

You might choose to label phrases and almost-phrases in a way that only you see fit, and that’s alright.

In fact, how someone perceives phrase divisions can lend a deeply personal dimension to their artistry, giving a voice to yet another aspect of their subjectivity.
