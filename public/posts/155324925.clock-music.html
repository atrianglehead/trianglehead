This is the first in a series of posts that will explore aspects of music composition that I regard as fundamental. Music means many things to me: a medium of expression, a metalanguage, a reservoir of knowledge, a method for self-discovery, a set of mathematical structures... it goes on. I’d love to share some of that with you by exploring how these aspects relate to the act of musical creation. As we progress, I hope that I am able to convey some of the beauty of music that still inspires me to learn and practice more each day.

The aim is to start from something so simple that it is easy to take for granted. From there, we can build up a vocabulary of interesting patterns and ways of combining them. To keep it from becoming a purely technical exposition, we’ll apply what we learn to more complex pieces of music and wherever possible, we’ll relate what we find to existing patterns, songs, artists and traditions.

The great thing about working with ideas is that we can do a lot of the work within our minds before testing the ideas practically.

If I were to tell you this is music, there’s a good chance you’re going to be unimpressed enough to stop reading immediately.

So I won’t tell you that. But I will show you that it can become music if you play around with the ideas hidden within it.

Composition, as I think of it, is a lot about making use of what you have, or more simply: it’s about playing around with what you have.

Also, even if a composition is never materialised or performed using the medium of sound, it can be a combination of ideas that can exist entirely within your mind, kind of like poetry or math.

Of course, that doesn’t mean that compositions need to be holed up within your mind and never see the light of day. I’m just trying to point out that it’s possible for you to compose music in a way that it remains as a collection of ideas within your mind while still giving you immense enjoyment. These ideas can then be painted onto the canvas of time, using sound.

So, the two claims I’m making are:

  1. Composition is about playing with what you have

  2. Composition can happen within your mind

To bolster these claims, we’re going to use this one recording and the ideas that it inspires, to make something musical.

Let’s take a closer listen to the one recording we have. Here it is again:

[audio]

To play with what you have, it helps to have a feel for it. Does something as simple as clock ticks make us feel anything? I don’t necessarily mean emotion. I’m talking about any and all sensations, physical, mental or otherwise that we may feel when we listen deeply to the above recording. Make a note of what you feel, even if it’s nothing. This will be useful later.

Personally, I feel a very tiny urge to move with every tick. Sometimes, it’s the urge to tap my foot and other times it’s the desire to slightly sway synchronously with each tick.

For those who felt no sensations, try tapping your hand or foot along with each tick, or simply sway to the clicks to see if that feels interesting.

What regularities or patterns do we notice when we hear the recording? Take a moment to think for yourself, while listening to the recording as many times as you need to, before going on.

The most obvious thing to me is how regularly the ticks follow each other. It feels like the same duration of time is separating one tick from another.

While I can’t claim that they’re exactly equally spaced apart down to the smallest interval of time, I can at least imagine that to be the case. This is valuable. Just being able to imagine an idealisation is a great first step in most situations. It helps us simplify complex sounds so that we can feel comfortable enough to play with them.

Ok. You may or may not have felt any sensations but I hope we can agree that that we now have a concept in mind: the ticks are spaced regularly in time. What can we do with it? Can we learn something new by exploring this one tiny concept that we have?

I think we’re getting somewhere. We started off with a recording. Then we observed an idea within it. We separated this idea from the recording in a way that we didn’t need to think about the recording any longer. Then we explored this idea to come up with new ideas.

And finally, we applied these new ideas to our original recording.

This is by no means the only way to do this. But it is a pretty common and useful method in exploring sound.

So what now? From one recording, we’ve managed to generate many more. I’ll call each of these a phrase. While they are recordings, let’s not forget that they each still represent an idea.
